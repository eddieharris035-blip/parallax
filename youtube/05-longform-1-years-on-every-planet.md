```
Here's something that'll mess with your head. If you were born today and lived an average human lifespan, you would never see a single Neptune year complete. Not one. The planet's orbit is so slow that Neptune has only made it around the sun one time since we discovered it back in 1846. One time. In almost two hundred years.

I started looking into how long a "year" actually is on every planet in our solar system, and the numbers got weirder the further out I went. So I built a free tool to calculate this for any birthday, and I want to walk you through what's actually going on out there. Because the physics of why some planets whip around the sun in three months while others take centuries to do the same thing is genuinely strange.

Let's start with what a year actually means. A year is one complete orbit around the sun. That's it. It's not 365 days because of some cosmic rule. It's 365 days because that's how long Earth specifically takes to go all the way around. Every planet has its own year length, and the speed of that lap depends on two things. How far the planet is from the sun, and how strong the sun's gravity is pulling on it.

The closer you are to the sun, the harder it pulls you, and the faster you have to move to keep from falling in. Out farther, the gravity weakens, and you can take your time. That's why Mercury rips around the sun in 88 days while Neptune crawls along for 165 of our years to do the exact same thing. The orbit isn't longer because the path is longer. The orbit is longer because the planet itself is moving slower. Way slower.

Let's go through them one by one. Each one has at least one detail that's going to throw you off.

Mercury. 88 days per year. That's about three Earth months. Mercury is so close to the sun, and moving so fast through space, that if you were standing on its surface, the sun would be a giant burning disc filling a massive chunk of the sky. But here's the truly weird part. Mercury has a year of 88 days, but a single day on Mercury, sunrise to sunrise, is 176 Earth days. That means one day on Mercury is twice as long as a year on Mercury. You'd celebrate two birthdays before the sun came back up.

Venus. 225 days per year. Venus is even stranger than Mercury when it comes to day length math. One Venus day, one full rotation, takes 243 Earth days. Which is longer than its year. Venus actually rotates so slowly that it completes an orbit around the sun before it completes a single spin on its axis. And on top of all that, Venus rotates backwards. The sun rises in the west and sets in the east. Nobody fully knows why. The best guess is that some massive collision billions of years ago flipped the whole planet upside down.

Earth. 365.25 days per year. That's our baseline, and that point two five at the end is the entire reason we have leap years. Every four years we add an extra day to February to catch up. Because if we didn't, our calendar would slowly drift out of sync with the seasons. After a few hundred years, Christmas would be in July, and the Fourth of July would be in winter.

Mars. 687 days per year. So a Martian year is just under two Earth years. This is why NASA missions to Mars are weirdly timed. They wait for what they call launch windows. These come around about every 26 months, when Earth and Mars are positioned just right so a spacecraft can take the shortest possible path between them. Miss the window, and you wait two years for the next one. And if you ever go live on Mars, your birthday only comes around once every two Earth years. You'd basically age in slow motion.

Jupiter. 12 Earth years per year. Twelve. If you were born when someone else was already 12 on Earth, you would both be celebrating your first Jupiter birthday on the exact same day. The whole concept of being a certain age stops meaning what we think it means out there. A Jupiter teenager would be in their nineties by Earth standards. A Jupiter toddler would already be older than your grandparents.

Saturn. 29 Earth years per year. The Cassini space probe orbited Saturn for 13 straight years before it ran out of fuel and was crashed into the planet. Thirteen years sounds like a long mission. But Cassini didn't see Saturn complete a single year while it was out there. Not even half of one. If you've personally lived through one full Saturn year, you're probably looking at your high school reunion. Two Saturn years and you're hitting retirement age.

Uranus. 84 Earth years per year. And here's the wild part about Uranus. The whole planet is tilted on its side. Like, almost completely sideways. So as Uranus orbits the sun, each pole gets 42 years of constant sunlight followed by 42 years of total darkness. Imagine the longest summer of your life lasting four straight decades. The sun never sets. And then immediately into a 40-year winter where the sun doesn't rise. Not once. For 40 years.

Neptune. 165 Earth years per year. This is where it gets truly insane. Neptune was discovered by astronomers in 1846. It completed its very first full orbit since being discovered in the year 2011. One single Neptune year, in the entire time humans have known that the planet exists. Nobody alive when Neptune was first spotted ever lived to see it return to the same spot in the sky. And nobody alive today will ever see two full Neptune years pass. The math just doesn't work for a human lifespan.

And then there's Pluto. Even though we demoted Pluto from full planet status back in 2006, it still takes the cake for the slowest year in the neighborhood. 248 Earth years per orbit. Pluto was discovered in 1930. It will not complete its first full orbit since discovery until the year 2178. Nobody who was alive to see Pluto's discovery will ever see it complete a year. None of us will. Probably nobody currently watching this video will either. Pluto's first full year as a known object in our solar system will happen long after every single one of us is gone.

So why does any of this matter beyond being weird trivia? Because it completely changes how you think about your own time on Earth. We measure our lives in Earth years because that's the only clock we know. It's the only one we've ever had. But if you'd been born on Mercury, you would already be 149 years old by now. On Mars, you'd still be a kid. On Jupiter, you'd barely be 3. On Neptune, you wouldn't even be a quarter of one year old yet. Same body. Same heartbeat. Same life. But the number you put on it depends entirely on which planet you happen to be standing on.

This is exactly what got me so deep into this rabbit hole that I went and built a tool for it. It's called Parallax. You enter your birthday one time, and it shows you your exact age on every single planet in the solar system. Plus the precise Earth date of your next birthday on each one. My next Mars birthday is two years out. My next Neptune birthday is in the year 2155. I will, unfortunately, not be attending. The tool is completely free. No signup. Link in the description below this video.

One quick bonus before we wrap up. If you think Neptune and Pluto are slow, exoplanets, planets orbiting other stars, get even crazier. Astronomers have found planets where a year is only 18 hours. The planet is so close to its host star that it whips around at insane speed. And on the other end of the spectrum, there are rogue planets drifting through interstellar space with no host star at all. Those planets have no year. None. Just permanent nighttime, forever, drifting through the dark.

Our solar system happens to have planets with orbital periods that range from three months to nearly three centuries. That's a wild range to be sitting inside of, and most of us never think about it because the only number we ever actually care about is 365.

So here's what I want you to do. Pull up Parallax. The link is in the description below. Plug in your birthday. And then tell me in the comments how old you are on Mars, and how old you are on Pluto. I'm putting out a new video like this every week, all built around the free tools I'm making over at Parallax.

Thanks for watching. I'll see you on the next one.
```
