## SCRIPT

### [00:00–01:30] HOOK + INTRO — GROUNDED visual register
**Pexels search keywords:** *person reading laptop window light*, *coffee morning thoughtful*, *grid paper notebook close-up*, *calendar pages soft focus*

There's a guy named Tim Urban who wrote one essay in 2014 that ruined a lot of people's relationship with time, in the best way.

He called it "Your Life in Weeks." The math was simple: if you live to 90, you'll be alive for exactly four thousand, six hundred and eighty weeks. He drew it as a grid. 52 columns wide, 90 rows tall. One little box for each week of your entire life.

Most people don't recover from seeing that grid. I didn't.

I read his essay, did the math on myself, and then I asked a question he didn't ask. What does this look like on Mars? On Jupiter? On Neptune? Because Earth weeks are an Earth-rotation artifact. Our entire calendar is local.

Today I'll walk you through what Tim Urban showed us — the math, the visual, why it lands — and then take it across the solar system. Because the answer to "how much time do I have?" gets weirder the further out you go.

Stick with me. This is going to mess with your head a little.


### [01:30–03:30] THE ORIGINAL LIFE IN WEEKS — GROUNDED
**Pexels search keywords:** *grid notebook hand writing*, *Sunday morning sunlight*, *birthday candles*, *summer evening porch*, *Christmas morning warm light*

Here's the original concept, in case you missed it.

Picture a grid. 52 columns, 90 rows. Your whole life on a single sheet of paper. If you're 30, about a third of the page is already filled in. If you're 40, almost half. If you're 50, more than half.

It's not the math that gets you. The math is your kid's homework. It's seeing it laid out — being able to look at your life and count it. Years compress in your memory. A whole decade can blur into "my thirties." Weeks don't compress the same way. A week is small enough to still feel real.

Tim Urban's other gift was counting *what's inside* the weeks. If you're 40, you have about 2,600 Sundays left. About 40 more summers. About 40 more Christmas mornings. About 40 more birthdays of people you love. You can count them all. They're countable.

That's the gut-punch. Not that life is short. That it's *finite in units you can name*.


### [03:30–05:00] THE QUESTION TIM URBAN DIDN'T ASK — TRANSITION
**Pexels search keywords:** *globe spinning slow*, *night sky stars*, *planets rotation animation*, *clock face close-up*, *earth from space rotating*

But here's where I got stuck after reading him. A "week" is seven Earth rotations. A "year" is one Earth orbit around the sun. A "Sunday" is a cultural overlay we put on the seven-day cycle.

None of those numbers exist anywhere else in the solar system.

So if Tim Urban's grid is so powerful because it makes time visible — what does the grid look like on a planet where time works differently?

I started running the math. Then I built a calculator so anyone could run it. Here's what came back.


### [05:00–06:30] LIFE IN MARS WEEKS — COSMIC
**Pexels search keywords:** *mars planet surface*, *red desert landscape*, *rover wheels dust*, *space probe rendering*, *cosmic time-lapse*

Mars first.

A Mars year is 687 Earth days — almost twice as long as ours. So if you're 40 Earth years old, you're about 21 Mars years old.

If you applied Tim Urban's logic to Mars — 90 Mars years × 52 Mars weeks per Mars year — you'd get about 4,680 Mars weeks for a "full Mars life." Same number. But each Mars week is 13 Earth days, not 7.

Which means a Mars year of weeks looks like this: half as many weeks per orbit, twice as long each. Mars Sundays are spaced thirteen days apart. Mars summers are about six Earth months long.

The math is the same shape. But every unit means something different.

### [07:30–09:00] WHERE A "WEEK" BREAKS — COSMIC
**Pexels search keywords:** *jupiter planet rotation*, *venus surface clouds*, *fast spinning top*, *long exposure star trail*, *gas giant atmosphere*

Now Jupiter. Jupiter is the biggest planet in the solar system, and it spins the fastest. A Jovian day — one rotation — is about 10 Earth hours. That's it.

Which means if you tried to apply our seven-day "week" to Jupiter, you'd be talking about a 70-hour stretch — less than three Earth days. By the time you say "happy Monday," you're already in Wednesday.

Now Venus. Venus does something almost no one knows about: it spins slower than it orbits. One Venusian day takes 243 Earth days. One Venusian year takes 225 Earth days. Which means on Venus, a single day is *longer than the year that contains it*.

There's no way to make a "week" mean anything on Venus. The whole concept dissolves.

This is the part that messed with me. Because it means our seven-day week — the unit Tim Urban used to make our lives feel countable — is not a fact about time. It's a fact about Earth. Specifically, about how fast Earth spins relative to how long it takes to go around the sun.


### [09:00–10:30] PLUTO — DEEP TIME — COSMIC
**Pexels search keywords:** *pluto distant icy*, *deep space exploration*, *new horizons spacecraft*, *vast empty cosmos*, *long galactic time*

And then there's Pluto.

Pluto takes 248 Earth years to orbit the sun. Two hundred forty-eight years. The United States hasn't existed long enough to make one full Plutonian year.

If you're 40 Earth years old, you're 0.16 Plutonian years old. Less than two Plutonian months. Almost no one alive on Earth right now has lived through one full Plutonian year, because most people don't make it to 248.

Pluto was discovered in 1930. As I record this, it has not yet completed a single orbit since the year humans first saw it.

So if you map Tim Urban's grid to Plutonian time — 90 Plutonian years × 52 Plutonian weeks — you get the same 4,680 weeks shape. But the actual Earth-time it represents is 22,320 years. Most "lives" on Pluto would have started before the pyramids and ended after we colonize Mars.

Time is the same idea everywhere. The numbers are wildly different.


### [10:30–11:30] THE SYNTHESIS — TRANSITION (cosmic fading to grounded)
**Pexels search keywords:** *contemplative window sunset*, *hands holding coffee thoughtful*, *sunset reflection face*, *warm grounding scene*

Here's what I take from this.

Tim Urban's essay was a gift because it made our time visible. Our 4,680 weeks. Our 2,600 Sundays. Our 40 Christmas mornings.

The cosmic extension is a gift for a different reason. It shows that the visibility itself is local. The numbers are real. *And* the units are arbitrary. Both are true.

What you do with that is up to you. But for me, holding both at the same time changed how I thought about a Sunday. Knowing it's countable, AND knowing the count is a story Earth tells. It made the Sunday feel sharper, somehow. Smaller and bigger at the same time.

The math says you have about 4,680 weeks if you're lucky. The cosmos says: those weeks are also ten thousand Jovian days. They are also twenty thousand Mercury orbits. They are also a fraction of a single Plutonian breath.

You contain all of it. In one human-shaped life.

## CapCut workflow (DIY pipeline — see `feedback_invideo_pipeline.md`)
1. **Fresh CapCut project per Short** — 1080×1920 vertical, 30 fps. Long-form is 16:9 horizontal, 30 fps.
2. **B-roll** — pull from Pexels using keyword lists per scene (above). Pixabay as fallback.
3. **TTS audio** — CapCut TTS, **same voice as Long-form #2** (note: voice consistency issue carries from #2 — once we've picked a CapCut TTS voice we stick with it from #3 onward).
4. **Text overlays** — Montserrat ExtraBold or Bebas Neue, white, drop shadow 60% opacity. Center-frame for hooks/numbers; upper-third for connective phrases. Fade-in 8-12 frames.
5. **Music** — CapCut royalty-free, "Cinematic / Inspirational" instrumental, 12 dB below TTS.
6. **Auto-captions** — CapCut AI Captions on the whole video, position high.
7. **Export:** Long-form 1080p MP4 16:9 30fps; Shorts 1080×1920 60fps.
8. **File names:**
   - Long-form: `parallax-longform-3-tim-urban-extended.mp4`
   - Shorts: `short-A-tim-urban-wrecked-me.mp4`, `short-B-venus-day-longer-than-year.mp4`, `short-C-pluto-not-one-orbit.mp4`
