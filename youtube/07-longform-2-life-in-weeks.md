## SCRIPT (paste into InVideo AI)

### [00:00–01:00] HOOK — [SHORT A source]
[VISUAL: black screen → "4,680" types in slowly → little boxes fill the screen around it]

If you live to 90 years old, you'll be alive for exactly four thousand, six hundred and eighty weeks.

Not 90 years that feels endless. Not 32,000 days that gets fuzzy. Four thousand, six hundred and eighty weeks. That's it. That's the whole budget.

Weeks feel different than years, don't they? You can count them. You can fit your whole life on a single sheet of paper. And once you see it that way — once it's a grid you can actually look at — something shifts. The math doesn't change. You do.

Today I'll show you what that grid looks like. What you've already used. What you have left. And the surprising thing nobody tells you about how the math actually plays out.

Stick with me. This one's worth the ten minutes.

---

### [01:00–02:30] THE GRID
[VISUAL: 52×90 grid drawing itself one cell at a time]

Picture this. A grid. 52 columns wide — one column for each week of the year. 90 rows tall — one row for each year of your life. That's your entire life on one page. Four thousand, six hundred and eighty little boxes.

This isn't my idea. A writer named Tim Urban put this online in 2014 and it kind of broke people. Not because the math is complicated — your kid could do the multiplication — but because of what it looks like when it's right in front of you.

Your childhood is the top eighteen rows. Your working career, if you retire at 65, is forty-three rows. Retirement, if you make it to 90, is twenty-five rows.

That's it. That's the whole map.

---

### [02:30–04:30] WHAT YOU'VE ALREADY USED
[VISUAL: top portion of grid shading gray as numbers appear]

So do the math on yourself. Your age times 52. That's how many boxes get shaded "already used."

If you're 30 — one thousand five hundred and sixty weeks gone. About a third of the page filled in.

If you're 40 — two thousand and eighty weeks. Almost half.

If you're 50 — two thousand six hundred. More than half.

This is the part where most people get a little quiet. Because nobody actually walks around feeling like their life is half over at fifty. Years feel slow. Weeks don't.

The reason is simple: years compress in your memory. Big chunks of your life become a blur of one or two memories — that's why summers as a kid felt like forever and your thirties felt like a long weekend. Weeks don't compress the same way. A week is small enough to still feel real.

---

### [04:30–06:30] WHAT YOU HAVE LEFT — [SHORT B source]
[VISUAL: highlighted dots — one per Sunday, then one per summer, then one per full moon]

Here's where it gets useful. Don't just count weeks. Count the things inside them.

If you're 40, you have about two thousand six hundred Sundays left, give or take. That sounds like a lot. It also means: of every ten Sundays you'll ever have... you've already used four.

Same math for summers. About 40 more, if you're lucky. That's it. Forty more times the air gets thick and warm and the evenings stretch out. You're already out of about half of them.

Same math for full moons. Christmas mornings. Birthdays of people you love. You can count them. They're countable. That's the whole point.

This isn't supposed to make you sad. It's supposed to make things visible. Most people pretend their time supply is infinite because counting feels uncomfortable. The grid forces you to count. And once you see the numbers... you stop spending Sundays the same way.

---

### [06:30–09:30] WHERE THE WEEKS GO — [SHORT C source]
[VISUAL: grid coloring by category — sleep blue, work red, phone purple, commute yellow]

Now the part that wakes people up. Where do those weeks actually go?

Sleep. About a third of every week. That's twenty-eight rows of your entire life-grid. Twenty-eight years asleep. Sleep is good — your brain literally cleans itself overnight — but it's a third of your life.

Work. Nine-to-five for 45 years is another eleven rows. Eleven years awake, at work.

Commuting. Average American spends about an hour a day commuting. Over 45 working years, that's two and a half rows. Two and a half years. Sitting in traffic. Going to work.

Phone. This one hurts. Average screen time is four and a half hours a day. Over fifty adult years, that's nine rows. Nine years. Just looking at a screen. Not counting the screen at work. Just personal.

Add it up: sleep, work, commute, phone. About fifty rows of your ninety. Already accounted for.

Which leaves you... forty rows. Forty years of awake, off-the-clock, off-the-screen, off-the-road life. That's the actual budget. That's what you're working with.

You don't have to like that math. But you should know it.

---

### [09:30–12:00] THE REFRAME
[VISUAL: grid shifting — "spent" portion fades, "remaining" portion lights up bright]

OK. Heavy math out of the way. Here's the part most people miss.

Seeing your life as a grid doesn't make it shorter. It makes it visible. Most regret comes from invisible time — the weeks that slip by on autopilot, the Sundays you don't remember, the summers that blur together. The grid doesn't speed time up. It slows it down. Because once you can actually see a week, you stop wasting it on autopilot.

A friend told me he started doing one real thing every Sunday — a real walk, a real meal, a real conversation. Just one. He said his Sundays now feel three times longer than they used to. He didn't add hours. He added attention.

That's the trick. The math says forty rows left. Your job is to make those forty rows feel like the slowest, fullest part of the page.

There's something beautiful in that. Forty years of intentional time is more than most people who lived to 90 actually got — because most people lived 90 years and only experienced about ten of them on purpose.

You can flip that ratio. The math is on your side, if you know it's there.

---

### [12:00–13:00] OUTRO
[VISUAL: full grid one more time, this time with current week pulsing in the middle]

So here's where I leave you. Pull up your calendar. Find this week's box on your imaginary grid. Just one week. Fifty-two of these per year. Four thousand, six hundred and eighty if you're lucky.

What's it going to be? What's it going to mean? Don't answer in your head — write it down. Make this one count.

If you want to actually see your grid mapped out with your real birthday, I built a tool that does it. Link's in the description. Takes about ten seconds. Might be the most useful ten seconds of your week.

Thanks for spending some of yours here. See you next time.

## CapCut workflow (POV / text-overlay format)
1. **Fresh CapCut project per Short** — 1080×1920, 30 fps.
2. **B-roll** — pull free clips from Pexels / Pixabay / InVideo's stock library matching each scene column above.
3. **Tool screen-recording segments** — use Windows Game Bar (Win+G) or OBS to capture clean Parallax tool runs, then trim to 5–10 sec clips.
4. **TTS audio:**
   - Generate each TTS line in CapCut TTS using the SAME voice as Long-form #1.
   - Drop each TTS clip onto the timeline at the matching scene.
5. **Text overlays:**
   - Montserrat ExtraBold (built-in) or upload Bebas Neue.
   - White, drop shadow 60% opacity.
   - Center-frame for hooks + numbers; upper-third for connective phrases.
   - Enter with quick fade-in (8–12 frames) — not zoom or bounce. Clean.
6. **Music** — CapCut library, "Cinematic / Inspirational" instrumental, 12 dB below TTS.
7. **Auto-caption** the whole thing (CapCut AI Captions). Position captions high.
8. **Export** 1080×1920, 60 fps, MP4.

   - `short-A-4680-weeks-pov.mp4`
